**City or country**: there is no universal right answer. The right choice depends on three variables (work mode, family situation, total budget). The 2020 health crisis reshuffled the cards with the rise of remote work and the neo-rural trend, without invalidating the assets of the city (services, jobs, transport) versus those of the countryside (space, quiet, housing budget). This article offers a complete comparison, a detailed budget arbitrage, three typical profiles and a method to decide, including the often overlooked third path: peri-urban living.

> **In brief:**
> 1. Housing in major cities is on average 30 to 50 percent more expensive than in the countryside.
> 2. Country living adds 200 to 500 euros per month to the transport budget.
> 3. Remote work has tripled active workers' interest in rural areas since 2020.
> 4. 81 percent of French people see the countryside as the ideal living environment (Ifop survey for Familles Rurales).

## City or country: a trade-off being reinvented

The 2020 health crisis reshuffled the cards. According to an Ifop survey for Familles Rurales, 81 percent of French people now see the countryside as an ideal living environment, and INSEE confirms a 15 percent increase in residential migrations to rural areas since 2020. Neo-rural life is settling into both imagination and statistics.

But the trend is not without setbacks. A study by Déménageurs Bretons indicates that 15 percent of post-Covid neo-rurals regret their choice, generally due to isolation, commutes or a poorly anticipated professional project. The right arbitrage therefore relies less on an idealised projection than on honest clarity about one's own priorities.

### Three typical profiles to find your bearings

To position yourself, three profiles dominate the trade-offs observed on the ground.

- **The committed urbanite** values local services, cultural outings and dense public transport. Often a young professional, an early-career executive or an active senior who refuses car dependency. Acceptable compromise: a smaller flat to stay central.
- **The semi-rural remote worker** seeks a balance between nature and professional connection. Hybrid remote workers (2 to 3 days at the office per week), executives and freelancers form the bulk of this group. Compromise: a house in a peri-urban area or a market town, accessible by train or short car ride.
- **The full neo-rural** assumes a clean break with the city. 100 percent remote freelancers, active retirees or families seeking space often settle far from employment hubs. Compromise: accepting reduced daily mobility and a more modest local life.

## City and country: the global comparison at a glance

The table below summarises the main trade-offs between a major city, a mid-sized city (20,000 to 100,000 inhabitants) and the countryside in 2026.

| Criterion | Major city (over 100,000 inhab.) | Mid-sized city (20,000 to 100,000 inhab.) | Countryside (under 2,000 inhab.) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Average price per sqm | €4,500 to €9,800 | €2,200 to €4,000 | €1,200 to €2,500 |
| Transport | Metro, bus, train | Bus, regional train, useful car | Mandatory car |
| Local shops | Walking distance | Walking or 5 min drive | 5 to 20 km away |
| Schools | Numerous | Solid network | Grouped (RPI) |
| Employment | Diverse | Solid local fabric | Limited, remote work |
| Air quality (PM2.5) | 12 to 18 µg/m³ | 8 to 12 µg/m³ | 5 to 8 µg/m³ |
| Crime rate (per 1,000 inhab.) | 60 to 90 | 30 to 55 | 10 to 25 |
| Fiber coverage | Over 95% | 80 to 90% | 55 to 75% |
| Social ties | Anonymity | Balanced local life | Strong community |

The mid-sized city plays an often underestimated pivotal role: it retains most urban assets while absorbing some of the country advantages (price, air quality).

## What monthly budget for which lifestyle?

For a couple with two children, the budget gap between the three zones remains the most structuring argument. The ranges below cover housing, transport and everyday services (sources INSEE and Notaires de France 2024-2025).

- **Major city**: €1,500 to €2,200 housing (rent or mortgage), €150 public transport (family pass), €250 to €400 services and childcare.
- **Mid-sized city**: €900 to €1,400 housing, €250 to €350 transport (1 to 2 cars, complementary regional train pass), €180 to €300 services.
- **Countryside**: €600 to €900 housing, €400 to €600 transport (2 cars almost mandatory, fuel, maintenance, insurance), €150 to €250 services and ancillary kilometric costs.

Net difference favours the countryside, between €300 and €800 saved per month depending on profiles. But the hidden cost of time remains massive: 1 to 2 hours of daily travel when the rural area is far from an employment hub, around 30 days per year spent in the car for 1.5 hours per day.

## How remote work changes the equation

Remote work has become the n°1 trigger for geographical trade-offs. According to several converging studies, around 30 percent of partial remote workers consider settling in the countryside or in a mid-sized city. But the decision requires checking three technical prerequisites.

- **Available fiber optic**: minimum 100 Mbps download speed, to be checked on the Arcep map (official fiber rollout map). Without fiber, check eligibility for fixed 4G or satellite.
- **Train station within 30 minutes by car**: essential for occasional returns to head office, especially for hybrid remote work (2 to 3 days on site).
- **Minimum 4G coverage**: mobile dead zones to avoid, as they compromise video calls, hotspots and professional availability.

Hybrid remote work mechanically pushes towards peri-urban areas rather than deep countryside. Before moving, it is also wise to check the employer's HR policy: some companies impose a partial return to the office, and a property project may then shift to another territory.

## The third path: living in a peri-urban area

The peri-urban area remains the great forgotten of the city-country debate. INSEE defines it as the ring of an urban area, 2,000 to 50,000 inhabitants, 15 to 45 minutes by car from the employment hub. This third path responds to a strong intent among working households.

Three major advantages drive the appeal of peri-urban living.

- **Price per sqm 2 to 3 times lower** than in the nearby metropolis, for more generous surfaces (garden, garage, study room).
- **Access to services**: schools, doctors, supermarkets, sports facilities are usually available in the market town or neighbouring city.
- **Public transport connection**: regional and suburban trains often provide a reliable daily link with the employment hub.

Three drawbacks moderate the enthusiasm.

- **Frequent daily commutes**, sometimes saturated at peak times.
- **Soulless housing estates**, with limited village life.
- **Strong car dependency** for leisure, shopping and accompanying children.

Concrete examples of active peri-urban areas in 2026 include the outer Paris ring (Mantes-la-Jolie, Melun, Étampes), the Lyon outskirts (Villefranche-sur-Saône, Vienne), the Nantes basin (Ancenis), and the rings of Toulouse, Bordeaux and Rennes. ## Criteria to decide based on your situation

Six criteria structure a rational arbitrage, to be assessed as a couple or family.

1. **Nature of work**: mandatory in-person, hybrid or full remote. Each case points to a very different type of zone.
2. **Age and needs of children**: nursery more accessible in urban zones, teenagers needing autonomous transport (regional trains, bike, bus) to gain independence.
3. **Available budget and debt level**: include the 35 percent maximum debt ratio and the total project cost (housing + transport + services).
4. **Proximity to loved ones**: childcare, parental support or family visits weigh heavily on mental load.
5. **Tolerance to commutes and noise**: a worker sensitive to urban noise will not have the same threshold as a long-time city dweller.
6. **Professional perspectives over 10 years**: internal mobility, local job market, partner's opportunities.

Before signing, complete the trade-off with a [buy-or-rent comparison](/en/blog/buy-or-rent-comparison/) and remember to factor in notary fees and acquisition costs into the total budget.

## False good calculations to avoid

Five mistakes systematically come up in poorly prepared city-country projects.

- **Underestimating commutes**: 1.5 hours of daily travel equals 30 days per year spent in the car, a full month of active life.
- **Psychological wear** of long commutes, barely visible the first six months but heavy over time.
- **Teenagers' needs**: they often suffer more from rural isolation than younger children, who are protected by the family cocoon.
- **Social isolation**: rural community is strong but slow to integrate, counter-intuitive for newcomers used to urban anonymity.
- **Idealising the countryside**: 15 percent of post-Covid neo-rurals regret their choice according to Déménageurs Bretons, mainly due to disconnection from daily reality.

### Method to validate your choice before signing

Six simple steps to secure a project, whether urban, peri-urban or rural.

1. **List five priorities** in order of importance, as a couple or family (space, budget, schools, commutes, services).
2. **Test your target area** over three weekends and a full week, including a rainy day to observe daily life.
3. **Time real commutes** at peak hours, not the off-peak Google Maps simulation.
4. **Talk to five settled residents** on site, not only the estate agent or developer.
5. **Simulate the full real budget** on a spreadsheet: housing, transport, services, subscriptions, childcare, sport, social outings.
6. **Check fiber, mobile coverage and the presence of a train station** if remote work enters the equation.

## Settlement aids: PTZ, Denormandie and rural schemes

Several public schemes make buying more accessible, especially in mid-sized cities and rural areas. Four levers structure the landscape in 2026.

- **The PTZ (zero-rate first-time buyer loan)**: from 1 April 2025 and until 31 December 2027, accessible everywhere in France, financing up to 50 percent of the property depending on the zone and household composition (€180,000 ceiling for new builds). Combinable with a classic mortgage.
- **The Denormandie scheme**: tax exemption for the purchase of an old property to renovate in 240 eligible municipalities, especially relevant for mid-sized cities engaged in town centre revitalisation.
- **Action Cœur de Ville**: programme covering 234 mid-sized cities, with renovation aid and favourable tax framework for buyers and landlords alike.
- **Rural revitalisation zones (ZRR)**: partial corporate tax and local tax exemptions in selected rural municipalities chosen on density and economic fragility criteria.

Conditions evolve regularly. Before any commitment, check eligibility and consult our guide to [home loan financing strategies](/en/blog/home-loan-financing/) to build a solid purchase plan.
