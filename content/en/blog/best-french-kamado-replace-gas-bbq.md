## Why replace a gas barbecue with a French kamado

The barbecue market in France has tripled between 2010 and 2024 according to the Federation of cooking industries, driven by the upmarket shift of charcoal models and the emergence of **ceramic kamados**. The kamado, a direct heir of the traditional Japanese oven, is the only **outdoor cooking tool** capable of replacing four appliances at once: a charcoal barbecue, a pizza oven, a smoker and a roaster. This versatility makes it the most cited alternative to replace an aging **gas barbecue**.

Three fundamental differences separate a kamado from a standard **gas barbecue**. The thick ceramic retains heat over several hours without reloading, opening up cooking modes impossible with gas: 110 °C smoking over 8 to 12 hours, low-temperature cooking such as pulled pork, sourdough bread, 350 °C pizza. Charcoal consumption is three to four times lower than a standard charcoal barbecue thanks to the thermal insulation of the **ceramic**. Finally, the lifespan of a quality ceramic kamado exceeds 20 years versus 5 to 10 years for a gas barbecue exposed to weather.

### Objective criteria to compare kamados

Seven criteria allow a rational comparison of **kamados available** on the French market:

- **Origin**: French, European or imported outside the EU. Origin influences customer service, after-sales support and logistics footprint.
- **Internal cooking diameter**: 47 cm for the standard large size. This is the usable cooking surface, independent of the external diameter.
- **Ceramic thickness**: between 25 and 38 mm. The thicker the ceramic, the better the thermal insulation and the lower the charcoal consumption.
- **Ceramic warranty**: lifetime on top-of-the-range models (COEO), 5 to 10 years on most brands.
- **Included accessories**: multi-level system, heat deflectors, thermometer, charcoal basket. Sold separately, they represent 200 to 400 euros.
- **Hinge**: stainless steel spring on top-of-the-range models, which compensates for the weight of the lid.
- **Side shelves**: HPL Compact (high pressure laminate) on the COEO, bamboo or teak wood on most other models. HPL Compact requires no maintenance, unlike wood.

## Practical advice to choose the right ceramic kamado

Five tips help secure a **ceramic kamado** purchase, regardless of the model chosen.

Define the size before the brand. The 47 cm internal diameter (large size) suits a household of 4 to 8 people. Below 4 people regularly, a medium model (35 cm internal) is enough. Above 10 people or for cooking large pieces (whole loin, leg, turkey), move up to the big format (54 cm internal).

Check the **ceramic warranty** before the global warranty. Brands often advertise a lifetime warranty that covers the structure but limit the ceramic warranty to 5 or 10 years. On a kamado, ceramic is what may crack (thermal shock, handling). The lifetime ceramic warranty from COEO is unique on the French market in 2026.

Compare the **delivered price including accessories**, not the kamado-only price. A Big Green Egg Large at 1,600 euros without accessories becomes 1,900 to 2,000 euros once equipped. The COEO Quatro Large at 1,249 euros ships with the multi-level system, deflectors and thermometer.

Plan the placement. A kamado weighs 80 to 120 kg in large size, charcoal not included. Once installed on the terrace, it rarely moves. Choose a location sheltered from direct water spray (heavy rain, automatic sprinkler), with at least 1 meter of working space around it for serving.

Test on a meal before major occasions. Mastering charcoal, ignition and temperature control takes two to three uses. A test cook (whole chicken or beef rib) before a family meal avoids surprises.

### Mistakes to avoid

1. Buying a steel kamado (such as Char-Griller Akorn) thinking you save money: heat does not hold, charcoal consumption doubles and lifespan drops to 5 to 8 years versus 20 plus for ceramic.
2. Underestimating **ceramic quality**: thin ceramic of 22 to 25 mm on low-cost brands cracks more easily under fast thermal swings (going from low to high cooking temperature).
3. Underestimating the cost of accessories not included: models with apparent lower entry prices end up more expensive once equipped.
4. Choosing a model too small to save money: a 35 cm internal medium saturates at 6 people and forces two-batch cooking, which degrades the experience.
5. Storing the kamado without a cover in winter: ceramic withstands frost, but water seeping into vents can damage metal parts (vent, hinge). A suitable cover extends lifespan.

## Additional comparison: ceramic versus steel on a kamado

Not all **kamados** are equal in terms of material. The table below summarizes the fundamental differences between **ceramic** kamado and steel kamado (such as Char-Griller Akorn, Pit Boss K22), often confused in mainstream comparisons.

| Criterion | Ceramic kamado (e.g. COEO Quatro Large) | Steel kamado (e.g. Char-Griller Akorn) |
|-----------|------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Thermal inertia | High, 8 to 12 hours without reload | Low, reload every 1 to 2 hours |
| Charcoal consumption | Reduced (3 to 4 times less than standard charcoal barbecue) | Standard to high |
| Useful temperature range | 90 to 350 °C | 90 to 280 °C |
| Typical lifespan | 20 years and beyond | 5 to 8 years |
| Ceramic or paint warranty | Lifetime at COEO | 5 to 10 years on paint |
| Weight | 80 to 120 kg | 30 to 50 kg |
| Entry price | From 1,000 € | 250 to 500 € |

The steel kamado looks cheaper to buy but does not stand the comparison on long low-temperature cooks, which are one of the main reasons to choose a **kamado** over a **gas barbecue**. For exclusive high-temperature grilling 5 to 10 times a year, steel may be enough. For versatile use (grilling, baking, smoking, roasting), ceramic remains essential.
